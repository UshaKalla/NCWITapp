Story:
One night, while mindlessly scrolling through Instagram reels in bed, you see an outfit that catches your attention. A woman stepped into the frame wearing a mid-length gown. It had a beautifully draped boat neck, structured three-quarter sleeves, and a subtle A-line silhouette that moved like liquid silk. It was exactly what you had been picturing in her mind for months.
You pause, mesmerized by how perfect it was, intending to tap the save icon, but then your phone buzzes with an incoming text. By the time you reply and tap back into Instagram, your feed has automatically refreshed.
For the past forty-eight hours, you had been thinking about that dress. You try searching "emerald green boat neck mid-length wedding guest dress" on Google, but the results were a sea of endless, generic options that looked nothing like the masterpiece you had seen.
You closed your eyes and visualized it again. You could see the exact curve of the neckline, the way the fabric pleated at the waist, and the specific shade of green that would complement the bridal palette perfectly.
You stared down at a blank notepad on the table, picking up a pen. "If only I could just draw what's in my head," you muttered to yourself, tracing a rough outline of the collar in the air. "If only there was some way to take this memory and use it to find the real thing."


Product opportunity gap:  People frequently encounter outfits in daily life while on online media, window shopping or running into people on the streets. Sometimes they can’t save, take a screenshot, picture, save or bookmark that outfit. People wish there was a way to search up clothing from their memory. 

Existing Solution 
Business to Business (B2B) Fashion Design Tools
Startups like Look AI and The Fabricant offer "sketch-to-image" tools. A fashion designer can doodle a rough line sketch, type "silk dress," and the AI renders a photorealistic image.
The Catch: These are expensive tools built for designers to make new clothes, not for consumers to find existing clothes. They don't look at a retail database to find out where to buy it.
Consumer Retail Search
Google Shopping (with its recent AI Mode) and apps like Pinterest have massive reverse-image search engines.
The Catch: As we discussed in your product gap, they are entirely dependent on you already having a real photo. If you didn't snap a picture, you can't use them.
The Gap My Product Fills 
My app takes the sketch-to-image tech from the designer world and plugs it directly into the marketplace-search tech of the consumer world. I am using generative AI to create a fake photo of a memory, just so a traditional search engine can understand what you're looking for.
